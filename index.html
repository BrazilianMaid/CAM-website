Point of View

Our view on channel

Most channel programs are built around recruitment. This one is built around revenue.

  • We do not believe in onboarding partners without a path to pipeline.
  • We invest heavily in enablement, co-sell, and support because partners cannot succeed without it.
  • We expect partners to generate pipeline within 90 days, and we support them to get there.
  • We prioritize partners who want to build a business, not just transact deals.
Section 1

Partner Management

Strong channel programs manage partners by lifecycle, not just logo count. Existing partners need strategic growth motions. New partners need fast activation, role clarity, and early wins.

Existing Partners Grow, retain, and expand

Our best partners are not transactional—they are extensions of our go-to-market team, driving pipeline, influencing deals, and managing the full customer lifecycle.

Primary objective

Increase sourced and influenced pipeline while improving partner stickiness, executive alignment, and product attach rates.

  • Quarterly business reviews with pipeline, renewals, and whitespace planning
  • Joint account mapping against priority territories and target accounts
  • Cross-sell and upsell plays across the product suite
  • Named CAM / channel coverage model with clear internal ownership
  • Partner scorecards tied to behavior, pipeline quality, and execution

Revenue creation process

1

2

3

Accelerate dealsSupport with SE alignment, pricing help, executive calls, and competitive strategy.

Reward growthUse incentives, MDF, access, and roadmap visibility for productive partners.
What “good” looks like: active executive sponsorship, repeatable pipeline creation, multi-threaded seller relationships, and visible services or recurring revenue attachment.
New / Onboarding Partners Recruit, activate, and get to first deal fast

Primary objective

We do not onboard partners for logo count. We onboard partners who are committed to building pipeline and closing business.

Every new partner enters a structured activation model designed to produce their first opportunity—and first deal—as quickly as possible.

  • Define an Ideal Partner Profile by size, route-to-market, vertical focus, and technical capability
  • Use targeted recruiting campaigns rather than broad “sign up” motions
  • Run executive alignment early to validate mutual commitment
  • Give each new partner a clear 30-60-90 activation plan
  • Measure time-to-first-meeting, time-to-first-opportunity, and time-to-first-dollar

Section 2

Partner Enablement & Co-Sell

Today’s winning programs focus on role-based enablement, co-sell execution, and repeatable motions that help both internal and external teams create pipeline.

Internal How direct teams and channel teams work together

Internal alignment priorities

  • Define when an AE should lead direct, with partner, or through partner
  • Clarify compensation alignment so partners are not viewed as conflict
  • Create a deal-conflict framework and escalation path
  • Train direct sellers on why each partner type matters in the sales cycle
  • Provide field-ready talk tracks, qualification guides, and co-sell plays

Internal pipeline creation process

1
Map partner coverage to field coverageAlign partners by territory, segment, vertical, and capability.
2
Choose account motionsPick named-account, territory, or campaign-based engagement models.
3
Launch co-sell playsRun structured plays with customer pain, target persona, and clear call-to-action.
4
Inspect resultsReview sourced vs influenced opportunities, win rates, and stage conversion.
External How partners learn, position, and sell

External enablement priorities

  • Role-based content for sellers, SEs, and services teams
  • Clear use cases by customer pain and buyer persona
  • Competitive messaging and objection handling
  • Fast access to pricing, scoping, and deal registration
  • Simple language around where the portfolio fits and why it wins

Partner-facing pipeline creation process

1
Lead with discoveryGive sellers a short qualification framework to uncover urgency and pain.
2
Position the suiteMove from single product talk tracks to platform and business outcome messaging.
3
Register the dealReduce friction with clear steps, deal protection, and support expectations.

Enablement rule: if a partner seller cannot explain the customer pain, business value, and next step in under two minutes, the motion is still too complicated.

Section 3

Resources

These are the assets a Channel Chief creates to drive engagement, improve seller confidence, and help partners progress opportunities.

Resource Purpose What it should include
“3 Simple Questions” Hook Give partner sellers a fast discovery framework that creates urgency in early customer conversations. Three customer-facing questions, follow-up prompts, ideal personas, and example pain signals.
Product Battlecard Help sellers position the product suite quickly and consistently. Quick facts, key value props, target customers, differentiation, competitive notes, and objection handling.
Deal Registration Guide Remove ambiguity around deal protection, timing, and internal routing. Eligibility, steps to register, approval flow, timelines, support coverage, and escalation contacts.
Scope & Pricing Guide Help partners qualify opportunities and move faster toward quoting. Packaging logic, pricing guardrails, scoping assumptions, services flags, and when to engage internal teams.
Partner Portal Create a central destination for engagement and execution. How to become a partner, training, register a deal, benefits, content library, support, and certifications.
First Deal Playbook Reduce time-to-first-revenue for newly onboarded partners. First target profile, sample outreach, discovery questions, demo path, deal support, and close plan.
Joint Account Planning Template Help both sides prioritize accounts and owners. Named accounts, owners, whitespace, mutual goals, target plays, and review cadence.
Partner QBR Template Create a repeatable operating cadence for existing partners. Revenue trends, pipeline health, top opportunities, blockers, enablement gaps, and next-quarter priorities.

Sample: “3 Simple Questions”

These are designed to hook a customer into a larger security conversation without forcing the partner seller into deep technical language too early.

  1. How are you identifying threats before they become incidents?
  2. What is your current time to detect and respond?
  3. If your current controls failed tomorrow, how quickly would you know—and who would own the response?

Sample: Battlecard Snapshot

  • Why we win: fast time to value, strong security outcomes, and clear business impact
  • What to say: lead with customer risk, operational burden, and measurable reduction in exposure
  • How to progress: qualify pain, register the deal, pull in the right support, scope cleanly, and advance to customer validation
Partner motion by type

What changes by partner segment

Different partner types require different economic stories, sales motions, and levels of operational support.

VAR priorities

  • Margin clarity and deal protection
  • Fast quoting and easy seller positioning
  • Competitive replacement plays
  • Account mapping with direct reps

What the program should emphasize

  • Deal reg simplicity
  • Battlecards and objection handling
  • Channel manager accessibility
  • Clear path from sourced lead to closed opportunity

MSP priorities

  • Recurring revenue and service attach
  • Multi-tenant manageability
  • Operational efficiency and automation
  • Packaged service creation

What the program should emphasize

  • MSP pricing and packaging
  • APIs, automation, and support model
  • White-labeled or branded service frameworks
  • Consumption and expansion economics

GSI priorities

  • Executive sponsorship and strategic alignment
  • Large transformation opportunities
  • Industry-specific solution plays
  • Joint IP or packaged offerings

What the program should emphasize

  • C-suite relationship maps
  • Vertical storylines
  • Solution integration points
  • Multi-region and enterprise account orchestration

Cloud / Marketplace priorities

  • Procurement simplicity
  • Private offers and consumption alignment
  • Joint field engagement with cloud sellers
  • Marketplace visibility and attach motions

What the program should emphasize

  • Cloud seller maps and joint pursuit plans
  • Marketplace packaging and listing readiness
  • Consumption-aligned messaging
  • Customer buying-path flexibility
